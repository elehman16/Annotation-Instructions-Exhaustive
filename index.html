Exhaustive Annotation Guide

This slideshow presentation will describe how you should approach the task.

A General Outline


This task involves assessing claims regarding particular interventions and outcomes that are made in biomedical articles. Specifically, you will be asked to indicate whether an article (which will describe the conduct and results of a clinical trial) reports evidence that agrees with a particular prompt stating the relationship between interventions and outcomes of interest. All evidence that details the relationship will need to be noted.

Example Prompt

All prompts will take the following format:

With respect to outcome, characterize the reported difference between intervention and comparator.

Examples of a real prompt would be:

With respect to frequency of headaches, characterize the reported difference between ibuprofen and control.

You will be asked to infer the answer to the prompt.

You will need to answer one of: significantly increase, significantly decrease, or no significant difference.
                  
Continuing with the previous example:
                  
If ibuprofen made headaches more common (in comparison to control), we would select "Significantly Increased."
                  
If ibuprofen made headaches less common (in comparison to control), we would select "Significantly Decreased."
                  
If ibuprofen made no difference in combatting headaches (in comparison to control), then we would select "no signficant difference."

Providing Support for Annotations


Once given the prompt and text, we will need to do two things:

  1. Determine the relationship between the intervention and comparator with respect to the outcome.
  2. Read the entire article, and mark wherever the answer is stated.

In the following slide, we will look at gathering "rationales" in the task (completing bullet #2). The example given only has the abstract; however, for the real task, you will need to mark up the entire article.

Providing Support

The prompt: with respect to overall mortality, characterize the reported difference between those taking decremental PEEP titration and those taking table-based PEEP (control).

Introduction

In acute respiratory distress syndrome (ARDS), adequate positive end-expiratory pressure (PEEP) may decrease ventilator-induced lung injury by minimising overinflation and cyclic recruitment-derecruitment of the lung. We evaluated whether setting the PEEP using decremental PEEP titration after an alveolar recruitment manoeuvre (ARM) affects the clinical outcome in patients with ARDS.

Methods

Fifty-seven patients with early ARDS were randomly assigned to a group given decremental PEEP titration following ARM or a table-based PEEP (control) group. PEEP and inspired fraction of oxygen (FiO2) in the control group were set according to the table-based combinations of FiO2 and PEEP of the ARDS network, by which we aimed to achieve a PEEP level compatible with an oxygenation target. In the decremental PEEP titration group, the oxygen saturation and static compliance were monitored as the patients performed the ARM along with the extended sigh method, which is designed to gradually apply and withdraw a high distending pressure over a prolonged period, and the decremental titration of PEEP.

Results

The baseline characteristics did not differ significantly between the control and decremental PEEP titration groups. Initial oxygenation improved more in the decremental PEEP titration group than in the control group. However, dynamic compliance, tidal volume and PEEP were similar in the two groups during the first week. The duration of use of paralysing or sedative agents, mechanical ventilation, stay in the intensive care unit and mortality at 28 days did not differ significantly between the decremental PEEP titration and control groups.

Conclusions

The daily decremental PEEP titration after ARM showed only initial oxygenation improvement compared with the table-based PEEP method. Respiratory mechanics and patient outcomes did not differ between the decremental PEEP titration and control groups.

A Clarifying Note about Significance.

The idea here, is that we only mark things as significant for p-values less than 0.05.

Prompt:
With respect to frequency of quit events by day 35, characterize the reported difference between patients receiving varenicline and those receiving placebo.

Text from article:
"By the end of the quit window (Day 35) 391 (80.5%) varenicline subjects and 121 (73.3%) placebo subects reported making a quit attempt (p = 0.062)"

Reasoning and Answer

Although the article suggests that varenicline subjects made more of an effort to quit than placebo, we mark the prompt with an answer of no significant difference, as the p-value stated is greater than 0.05.

Working with Clinical Significance.

The idea here is that we want to clarify what to do when conflicting information is presented (concerning clinical vs. statistical significance).

Text from Article:

"There was a significant difference n mean hemoglib and ferritin levels in Group B at 38 weeks (P = 0.018 and P = 0.035, respectively), but this difference was not clinically significant (hemoglobin >12g/dL, ferritin >19 ug/L)."

Answer

Based on the table on the right, we must select an answer of no significant difference.
